1.3 European Exploration in the Americas

Reasons

- population increase after Black Plague

- political unification

- desire for luxury goods

- trade w/ Asia had been taken over by Muslims


- Henry the Navigator (Portugal) established trading post empire around Africa, dominated Indian Ocean

- harnessed technology: caravel ships, charts, astrolabe

- Spain, jealous of Portugal, set up trading posts to spread Christianity under Isabella & Ferdinand

- Christopher Colombus got permission to sail west

- discovered New World, thought it was East Indies

- set off Colombian Exchange


1.4 Columbian Exchange

Columbian Exchange

- transfer of food, canimals, minerals, people, diseases, between Africa, Europe, Americas


1. Disease

- smallpox from Spanish ravaged natives, allowed conquest

- Arawak, Tainos devastated on Hispaniola

- Incas, Aztecs, Mayas decimated

2. Food

- to Europe: maize, tomatoes, potatoes, cacao, tobacco

- to Americas: rice, wheat, soybeans, rye, oats, lemons, oranges

3. Animals

- to Americas horses, pigs, cattle, chicken (revolutionizing)

4. Minerals

- gold & silver plundered by Spaniards

5. People

- Africans enslaved to the New World

Western Europe grew rich from income

- land system changed from feudalism to capitalism


Note: Exploration financed by Spanish state and mercantilist policies

- mercantilism depended on government direction

- differed from later exploratioon financed by joint-stock companies


1.5 Labor, Slavery, and Caste in Spanish America

Slavery

- Europeans settled along West African coast, trading slaves for guns

- Justified slavery through black inferiority

- used Biblical justifications

- Spanish Requerimiento demanded natives to accept Spanish rule and Catholicism to gain protection

- encomienda system used to extract native labor

- Africans quickly replaced natives (more immune, less familiar w/ land)


New Social Order (for tax system):

1. Peninsulares (born on Iberian peninsula)

2. Criollos (Spanish born in America)

3. Mestizos (Spanish x Native)

4. Mulattoes (Spanish x African)

5. Africans and Natives


1.6 Cultural Interactions Between Europeans, Natives, and Africans

- Spanish dominated interactions (hegemony)

- began sending missionaries to convert natives

- mission system


Cultural differences

Natives Spanish
- Pantheists/Animists - Catholic
- land not a commodity - land meant to be sold
- large kinship networks - nuclear family

- natives wanted Spanish tools, horses, guns

- Europeans wanted native fur trade

- married natives to gain rights


Pueblo Revolt

- Pueblos forcibly converted and troubled

- Pueblos killed 400, burned missions


Bartoleme de las Casas defended native religious freedom

- suggested Africans replace them


Summary / Main Takeaways

- very diverse native structure before Europeans arrived

- dealing with competition, Spain funded Colombus' voyage west

- trading of diseases (decimated Natives), animals, and peoples occured in the New World

- labor among both natives and Africans thrived

- Spanish hegemony in the New World

2.3 — The Regions of the British Colonies

Regions of the British Colonies:

- enslaved blacks used to replace angry indentured servants


New England Colonies

- migrated as families

- economy centered on agriculture + commerce


British West Indies // Southern Atlantic

- 1620s: colonies settled on St. Christopher, Barbados, Nevis

- majority black populations


- chattel slavery replicated in South Atlantic


Middle Colonies

NY, NJ

PA

2.4 — Trans-Atlantic Trade

trade became global in late 17th–early 18th c.


Triangular Trade

- three-part trade journey between Europe, Americas, and Africa

- goods from Britain exchanged in West Africa for slaves

- slaves shipped through Middle Passage to the West Indians for sugar and rum

- sugar and rum sold to Britain


What drove the Triangular Trade:


Mercantilism

- economic model of Europe

- fixed amount of wealth in the world

- export more than imports to gain as much wealth as possible

- establishing colonies (gain raw materials + new markets for their goods)


Navigation Acts

- helped interconnect Britain's colony network

- restricted colonial trade to England


Effects:

- new trade created thriving cities

- consumer revolutions

- place in society tied more to financial success than family lineage

Unit 3 (1754–1800)

3.2 French & Indian War

French & Indian War

- the American theatre of the global Seven Years War

- French (and allied Indians) vs. British, fight for North American territory


Cause

- Brtish colonists encroaching on French land, territorial disputes

- scuffle over the French Fort Duquesne led by George Washington


- Albany Congress discussed defense efforts and unionization, proposed Albany Plan of Union

- consisted of delegates from British colonies including Iroquois

- early predecessor to future conventions

- Indians allied w/ both French and British to gain control


Colonial resentment

- impressment (forced recruitment) of soldiers

- troops quartered


- Treaty of Paris ended War (1763)

- Florida ceded to British from Spain

- land east of Mississippi given from French to Britsh

- land west of Mississippi given from French to Spansh


Effects

- colonists pushed west, conflicting with natives

- Pontiac's Rebellion led by natives fought new settlers

- Proclamation of 1763 set Appalachian Mountains as limit for settlers to avoid native conflict

- increased resentment toward British

- debt increased from War, resulting in taxes

- increased resentment toward British


3.3 Taxation without Representation

Salutary neglect

- certain laws became unrestricted on Americans due to distance between Britain


Grenville's Plan to regain control

1. stricter enforcement of laws (Navigation Acts)

2. extend wartime provisions into peacetime

3. Quartering Act of 1765


Various taxes

Sugar Act

- coffee, wine, taxes

- enforced existing taxes on molasses

Stamp Act

- paper items

Currency Act

- prohibited own currency


"No taxation without representation" movement arose

- Enlightenment political ideas pushed the effort (see 3.4)


- British argued that taxing the colonists was a form of virtual representation

- virtual representation: Parliament members in Britain represented colonists

- angered colonists who thought they should be represented by people who lived in the colonies


Resistance:

- Sons of Liberty, Daughters of Liberty resisted British

- Stamp Act Congress petitioned Parliament for repeal

- Parliament repealed Stamp Act and Sugar Act


- Declaratory Act (1766) declared British could pass anything they want

- Townshend Act (1767) taxed paper, tea, glass

- many continued boycotting taxes, tensions rising

- Boston Massacre (1770): scuffle between British and coloonists saw 4 colonists dead

- Boston Tea Party (1773): in response to Tea Act, colonists dropped 45 tons of tea in Boston harbor


Coercive Acts/Intolerable Acts (response to Boston Tea Party)

- Boston Harbor closed until tea paid for

- a second Quartering Act was passed


3.4 Philosophical Foundations of the Revolution

Continental Congress (1774)

- colonial delegates met to discuss British legislative tyranny

- did NOT discuss revolution—still identified as British subjects and wanted compromise


Enlightenment influences

John Locke

- argued for consent of the governed

- argued for natural human rights (life, liberty, property)

- argued for self-rule through representation

Jean-Jacques Rosseau

- argued for a social contract (people consent to laws)

Montesquie

- argued for Executive, Legislative, and Judicial government branches


from a desire for compromise to a desire for independence...


- Thomas Paine's "Common Sense" bashed on Britain

- simple language made it easy to understand for colonists

- Second Continental Congress (1776)

- directly called for independne

- Thomas Jefferson composed Declaration of Independence


3.5 The American Revolution

not all agreed

- many neutral

- Loyalists resisted Patriots


- George Washington appointed General of Continental Army

- poorly armed, poorly trained—rough army

- British deployed 10,000 troops—60,000 Loyalists joined them


War strategy

- Colonists fought war of attrition, tiring British out

- 5,000 blacks joined ranks


Important battles

- after crossing Delaware, Washington's troops attacked Hessians (British-allied Germans)

- Battle of Saratoga (1777)—American victory

- this battle encouraged French to join American cause

- French eager to weaken their enemy, Britain

- Spain and Holland also joined American cause

- Battle of Yorktown (1781)—American victory (aided by French)

- British surrendered


End of War

- Paris Peace Treaty (1783) ended War

- Britain recognized United States as a nation w/ western boundary at Mississippi River


3.6 The Influence of Revolutionary Ideals

How the ideals of the Revolution influenced society...


In America:

- talk of liberty influenced disucssion of slavery

- northern states abolished slavery

- Continental Congress abolished slave importation

- slavery continued in South

- greater democracy within nation

- universalized suffrage without respect to titles/nobility

- greater respect for women

- "Republican Motherhood" reflected how mothers raised good patriots


Globally:

- inspiring American Revolution influenced other revolutions

- inspired French Revolution

- "Declaration of the Rights of Man" provided for limited monarchy in France

- Haitian Revolution

- enslaved in Haiti rebelled, established independent nation
