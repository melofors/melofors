Understanding Spectral Data

Spectral data is a record of the energy or radiation emitted by celestial objects across various wavelengths on the electromagnetic spectrum. This spectrum spans from visible light, which our eyes perceive, to X-rays, infrared, ultraviolet, and even radio waves.


A spectral emission graph of the Sun. The "spectrum" which the data follows is shown below it, hence the word used, "spectral".

The intensity and pattern of radiation at different wavelengths carry a wealth of crucial information about the object's chemical composition, temperature, velocity, and magnetic fields. By deciphering these spectral signatures, astronomers unveil the secrets of galaxies, stars, and cosmic phenomena.

Thus, these complex characteristics can be measured from all the way home, without needing to ever measure directly.

The Science of Spectra

Elements in celestial objects absorb or emit radiation at different radiations, resulting in peaks or dips in the resulting spectra. Scientists have studied specific elements and know which wavelengths of radiation they tend to absorb or emit---this can help identify elements present in a spectral graph.

Above: the absorption and emission spectra of a hydrogen atom

When the atom absorbs light, the electron jumps to a higher energy level. The energy required for this jump results in a dip in the graph at that wavelength, resulting in hydrogen's signature absorption lines seen above.

Measurement

Spectrographs are instrumental devices used in telescopes and observatories to analyze the spectral data obtained through spectroscopy. They work by dispersing light into its constituent wavelengths and capturing the resulting spectrum. Powerful spectrographs are used in observatories and telescopes, such as the James Webb Space Telescope's two spectroscopic imaging instruments, NirCAM and MIRI.

Glass prisms disperse light into different wavelengths, similarly to a spectrograph


Selected Discoveries

Spectral Shift Reveals Puzzling Quasar: Astronomers used spectroscopy to discover a mysterious quasar in a nearby galaxy called NGC 7319. This quasar's light suggested it was billions of light-years away, creating a cosmic paradox since NGC 7319 is only 300 million light-years away. This finding challenges previous assumptions about quasars and their role in measuring distances in the universe, suggesting that current cosmological models may need revision.

Discovery Alert: Webb Maps and Finds Traces of Water in an Ultra-hot Gas Giant's Atmosphere: Using spectroscopy with NASA's James Webb Space Telescope, astronomers discovered water vapor in the atmosphere of an intriguing exoplanet, WASP-18 b. They also found temperature variations and indications of a strong magnetic field affecting its winds, providing insights into how such unique planets form.


The analysis of these patterns to unravel the intricate mysteries of the universe is an art form unlike any other—and one that should be accessible by anybody.


Data Analysis Tools --- jdaviz

To assist with incoming data from the James Webb Space Telescope as well as from other missions, spectral imaging tools have been made available by the team at the Space Telescope Science Institue.

  • Cubeviz: A tool for visualization and analysis of 3D data cubes from integral field units (IFUs).
  • Specviz: A tool for visualization and quick-look analysis of 1D astronomical spectra.
